When you have finished reading the essay “Supply-Side Economics: The Foundations” and listening to the lecture “Supply Side Economics, Say’s Law, and Economic Growth,” you should understand the following:

 

• Supply-side economics is not new

            These basic principles go back to the beginnings of modern-day economics,

            Adam Smith An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations          (1776)

           

            Jean-Baptiste Say, A Treatise on Political Economy (1803).

 

• John Maynard Keynes

             The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (1936),

                        Took economics in a different, misguided direction:

                        Removed prices and incentives in the private sector as the key factors

                        directing the economy and "supply" of goods and services

 

            Replaced price and incentives with aggregate "demand"

                        Government spending now seen as the way to fine tune the economy.

 

• Supply-side economics reveals the weaknesses and errors of Keynesian economic thought

 

• Supply-side economics:

            Places supply over demand in the hierarchy of the economics of the marketplace

            Deals with enhancing economic production, efficiency, and growth           

            Focuses on              

                        incentives for working,

                        incentives for saving

                        incentives for investing, and

                        incentives for risk-taking

 

• Supply creates demand. 

            There was no general demand for televisions, home computers, until someone   invented them.

 

            Often referred to as “Say’s Law,” after French economist Jean-Baptiste Say.

 

• General public policy principles or "Fiscal Levers" rooted in supply-side economics include:

            low marginal tax rates

            light government regulation of private enterprise

            limited government spending

            strong property rights

            free trade

            sound, anchored monetary policy

 

●Production

            • When production goes up, consumption is increased.

            •Production is the key ingredient to prosperity and economic growth consumption           follows.

 

            "Consumption drives the economy" is

                        The antithesis of Say’s Law

                        Consumption is the effect, not the cause of growth.

 

• The entrepreneur--the ultimate source of economic growth.

                        Entrepreneurs  fulfill the unseen needs of the consumer in the future.

                        Entrepreneurs–not government–drive the economy.

                        Source of innovation,

                        Source of new and improved goods and services

                        Source of new businesses

                        Source of entirely new industries
